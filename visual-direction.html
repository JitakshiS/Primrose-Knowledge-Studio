Visual Direction · for Jitakshi

Two paths for Knowledge Studio

Two distinct visual directions that move us away from anything editorial or templated. Both are modern, audience-first, and built to feel like a $29/month platform a Gen Z subscriber would happily pay for. Each is shown the way it would actually live — sales page, type pairing, palette, and how the 5 Pillars get treated. Pick one, I'll build the sales page in that direction first thing once the deposit lands.

Non-negotiable · 01
No serif headlines anywhere in the UI. Sans-serif with character, not generic Inter alone.
Non-negotiable · 02
16px rounded corners on cards, images, video frames, containers. Never sharp.
Non-negotiable · 03
Real photography of Primrose anchors the brand. Not stock, not illustration.
Non-negotiable · 04
Monospace details where they add flavour — timestamps, pillar labels, file metadata.
Non-negotiable · 05
Asymmetric layouts where they help conversion or comprehension. Not Bootstrap defaults.
Non-negotiable · 06
Audience-first, not client-first. Designed for the 26-year-old paralegal, not Primrose's law-firm aesthetic.
Direction A

STUDIO

A library you joined intentionally — warm, intellectual, considered.

Deep forest green and warm cream ground the brand in something that feels like a private studio you settle into. Sienna accents add warmth and confidence without going corporate. Bricolage Grotesque (variable, organic, characterful) does the heavy lifting in display — it's distinctive enough to feel hand-picked, not generated. Reads as premium, trustworthy, designed by someone who cares about typography. Closest energy: Substack's premium creator tools meets Maven meets an indie newsletter you'd happily pay for.

Direction B

CHANNEL

A platform that reads like it was built by people who care — confident, modern, designed.

Off-white paper, ink that's nearly black, and one confident clay-orange accent that punches through every page. Geist (Vercel's open-source sans, geometric but warm) at heavy weights does most of the work — it's the kind of type pairing you see on Linear, Cal.com, Vercel marketing. Modern, distinctive, unmistakably designed. The five pillars are treated as a numbered framework — bold, structural, the kind of system Gen Z reads as serious. Closest energy: Linear's marketing meets Cal.com meets Lemon Squeezy's premium tier. Less warm than Studio, more "this is a real product."

Recommendation

I'd lead with Studio.

Both directions clear the brief. Both feel modern, distinctive, and unmistakably not made by an AI template. The question is which one fits the audience and the product better — and on that, Studio wins for me.

My pick · Direction A

STUDIO — because trust is the conversion lever, and warmth carries trust better than precision.

Primrose's audience is anxious, mid-decision, and one design choice away from "not yet." Channel reads premium and confident — but premium can also read corporate, and corporate raises the bar she has to clear to be trusted. Studio reads warm and considered, which lowers the bar. The Bricolage display gives every page a hand-picked, human feeling that no AI template lands on. The forest-green + sienna palette has enough confidence to feel paid, enough warmth to feel approachable, and enough character to be unmistakably its own thing. It's the direction that says "a person made this, and you can trust the person teaching."

When I'd switch to Channel instead

If the launch strategy leans hard on virality (TikTok-first, viral hooks, growth over warmth), Channel's bolder type and structural pillar framework would punch harder in screenshots and create more recognisable Brand moments. The numbered framework treatment also reads more "Knowledge Studio OS," which matches Arlan's playbook language. Worth considering if the priority is shareable identity more than trust-led conversion.
